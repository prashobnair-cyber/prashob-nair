Index funds, always

July 2026 · 4 min read

Every few months someone asks me for a stock tip. I never have one, and that's not false modesty — it's the whole point.

I've spent a career around technology companies, sat in rooms where product roadmaps and competitive positioning got dissected in real time, and I still wouldn't bet my own money on picking the next winner. Not because I don't understand the businesses. Because the market already prices in what I know, and a lot of what I don't.

The evidence on this isn't close. Over any 15-to-20-year stretch, the overwhelming majority of actively managed funds fail to beat a broad market index after fees. The ones that do beat it rarely do it twice in a row. Survivorship bias hides the losers; recency bias makes us forget them. Meanwhile a low-cost global index fund just sits there, owns a little of everything, and lets the aggregate growth of the world's economy do the work.

My default recommendation, to friends and to my own kids eventually, is eMAXIS Slim All Country. It's not glamorous. It won't make a good story at dinner. It owns thousands of companies across developed and emerging markets for a fraction of a percent in fees, and it rebalances itself as the world's economic weight shifts. I don't have to guess whether the US stays dominant or whether India or Vietnam's growth story plays out — the fund just tracks it.

The hard part was never the strategy. It was accepting that "boring and diversified" beats "smart and concentrated" over the time horizons that actually matter. I had to unlearn the instinct — probably common to anyone in tech — that more research and more conviction should produce better outcomes. In investing, conviction is often just a more expensive way to be wrong.

There's a psychological cost to this too, one people don't talk about enough. Buying an index fund is unsatisfying in the moment. There's no thesis to defend, no insight to feel clever about. You just contribute, on a schedule, and let two decades of compounding do something no amount of stock-picking skill reliably does. The reward is deferred and undramatic, which is exactly why so few people stick with it when a hot sector is making headlines.

I think in decades, not quarters. RSUs vest, property values move, tax rules change, but the index fund keeps doing the same unremarkable thing in the background — quietly becoming the largest and steadiest part of the plan. If you're starting out and want one piece of advice that doesn't need a follow-up meeting: pick a broad global index fund, automate the contribution, and stop checking it so often.
