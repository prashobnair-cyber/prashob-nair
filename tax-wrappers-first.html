Max your tax wrappers first

July 2026 · 4 min read

Before I buy anything else, I fill the tax-advantaged accounts. NISA first, then iDeCo. Every year, without exception, before a single yen goes into a taxable brokerage account.

The logic is simple enough that people underrate it. NISA lets your investment gains grow and get withdrawn completely tax-free — no capital gains tax, no dividend tax, within its annual limits. iDeCo goes further: contributions reduce your taxable income now, growth is tax-deferred, and you get preferential tax treatment on withdrawal later. Skipping these and investing in a regular taxable account first is like being offered a discount at checkout and choosing to pay full price instead.

I call it free money because that's what it is. The government isn't doing this out of generosity — it wants citizens and residents building retirement savings so it isn't the only safety net later. But the incentive is real regardless of the motive, and it doesn't come back around if you don't use it. NISA has annual contribution limits that reset each year and don't carry over. Miss this year's allowance and it's gone, not deferred.

The mistake I see most often, especially among people newer to Japan, is treating this as complicated paperwork to get to eventually. It isn't. Opening a NISA account takes an afternoon. The harder part is psychological: watching a chunk of income disappear into an account you can't easily touch, especially with iDeCo, which locks funds until 60. That illiquidity is a feature for people who'd otherwise raid their savings, but it means you shouldn't put money in that you'll need for a house down payment or school fees in the next few years.

My sequence, in order, looks like this: emergency fund first, always. Then NISA up to the annual limit, in a broad index fund rather than anything exotic — no point paying tax-free on a fund that underperforms the market anyway. Then iDeCo, sized to what I'm genuinely comfortable locking away until retirement. Only after all of that do I look at a taxable account, and even then it's the same underlying index fund, just without the tax shelter.

None of this requires market timing or conviction about any particular stock. It requires discipline about sequencing and the patience to let a structural tax advantage compound for twenty years instead of two. If you take away one thing from how I think about money in Japan: the wrapper matters as much as what's inside it. Fill NISA and iDeCo before you do anything else.
