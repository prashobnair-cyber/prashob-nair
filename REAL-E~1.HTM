Real estate as tax strategy

July 2026 · 5 min read

I don't buy Japanese investment property because I think property values will rise. Most of the analysis I've seen suggests otherwise outside a handful of central Tokyo wards. I buy it because of what depreciation does to my tax bill.

Japan lets you depreciate a building's value against your income over a schedule tied to its structure and age. Wooden properties depreciate fastest, over as little as four years for older builds bought near the end of their statutory useful life. That depreciation is a paper loss — you're not actually losing money, the building isn't evaporating — but it offsets real income on your tax return. For someone in a high income bracket, that paper loss can meaningfully reduce what you owe for several years running.

This is the part people miss when they hear "real estate investment" and picture rental yield or price appreciation. The return here isn't really coming from the property. It's coming from your tax bill, funneled through the property as a vehicle. The rental income is almost secondary — nice if it covers the mortgage, not the point of the exercise.

The part that requires real attention is the cliff date. Depreciation isn't indefinite. Once the schedule runs out, the paper losses stop, and often what follows is a stretch where you're recognizing depreciation recapture — meaning some of what you deducted earlier gets taxed on the way out, frequently at a less favorable rate than what you saved going in. If you don't plan for that cliff, you can end up owning a property that flips from tax shelter to tax liability overnight, and worse, you're the one who has to notice the date arriving, because nobody sends a reminder.

My rule is to know the exit before I know the entry. Before buying, I map out the full depreciation schedule, the year the shelter effect ends, and what my income and tax bracket are likely to look like at that point. If the plan is to sell before the cliff, I want a realistic view of what the property will actually fetch, not an optimistic one. If the plan is to hold through the cliff, I want to already understand the recapture math so it isn't a surprise.

This only works as a strategy for people with meaningfully high taxable income to offset — it isn't a general recommendation the way index investing is. But if that describes your situation, real estate in Japan can be one of the more effective structural tools available, provided you treat it as a tax instrument with an expiration date, not a growth asset. Understand your cliff dates before you sign anything.
